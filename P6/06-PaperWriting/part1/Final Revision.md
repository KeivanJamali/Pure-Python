## 3. Examining the Impact of Waste on the Environment

One of the most important challenges caused by urbanization and infrastructure development, is construction and demolition (C&D) waste[1]. While the construction sector is essential for economic growth, the impact of waste is not negletable, which means we need to have a proper waste handling[2, 3]. Water and soild pollution, accelerate the depletion of natural resources, and increase in greenhouse gas emissions are the disasters that had been created during the lifecycle of construction materials by improper handling of waste[4, 5]. In recent studies, the impact of construction waste extends beyond just landfill occupation, emphasizing the real effects on ecological and climate-ralted systems[4, 5].

### 3.1 Water and Soil Pollution

One of the largest direct environmental effect caused by construction waste is the pollution of soil and water resources[6]. While there are methods to separate and do treatment on waste materials to protect landfills, many dispose of them without adequate treatment and separation in landfills or illegal dumping sites. This will lead to leach of hazardous substances into surrounding ecosystems causing environmental damage in the long term[6]. The frequent substances in waste are fine particles, heavy metals, paints, gypsum residues, solvents, and alkaline compounds which can degrade soil quality and contaminate groundwater if improperly disposed of[6].

There are several studies that have shown that the risk of heavy metal leaching into nearby soils and aquifers increases by uncontrolled landfill disposal[7]. Coating materials, metallic residues, treated wood, and concrete dust can create toxic leachates due to rainfall, which can transport these pollutants into groundwater systems and damage agricultural lands and nearby ecosystems[7].

Another problem caused by poor construction waste management is surface water pollution. Studies reported that illegal dumping and bad storage of construction debris let suspended solids and contaminated runoff to flow in urban drainage systems, rivers, and nearby water bodies[7]. This increase in turbidity and chemical contamination can hurt aquatic ecosystems while reducing water quality as well. Long-term ecotoxicity problems is shown in studies that can be created in local environments due to unmanaged waste disposal sites.

Life cycle assessment (LCA)-based research indicates that by recycling and sorting waste, environmental contamination risks can be reduced substantially compared with traditional landfill disposal methods[5]. Therefore, the solution to minimize toxic leachates and soil contamination, is separating hazardous materials and diverting recyclable waste from dumping sites.

### 3.2 Increased Consumption of Natural Resources

To throw away construction waste, there will be a need to replace them with other natural resources. As the need for raw materials increases over time, discarding construction waste is not the best option due to the valueable contents such as sand, gravel, limestone, clay, timber, and metals. Therefore, excessive consumption of natural resources will be caused by not reusing these materials[4].

Researchers have shown that the inefficient material management leads to the unnecessary deletion of non-renewable resources. Construction waste includes materials with high potential for reuse and recycling like concrete, bricks, asphalt, steel, and timber. Therefore, by not reusing these materials, we force ourselves into further quarrying, mining and extraction which lead to more environmental degradation[16].

Recent studies using life-cycle assessment (LCA) methods have shown recycling construction waste can highly reduce the extraction of virgin materials. Recycled concrete aggregates can be used in road construction and structural applications[8]. Likewise, steel recycling can also decrease the demand for iron ore extraction and reducing energy consumption during the manufacturing process.

Current research also highlights the transition from a linear construction model toward a circular economy approach[14, 15]. Instead of the linear "take-make-dispose" pattern, the use of modern strategies focused on designing buildings for material recovery, adaptability, and long-term reuse is suggested. Digital technologies like Building Information Modeling (BMI), material passports, and smart waste tracking systems are increasingly being adopted[10, 11]. As a result, on-site waste soring, resource recovery throughout the building lifecycle and material efficiency can be facilitated and maximized.

All being said, literature indicates that sustainable construction waste management is not limited to simple recycling practices and has become an essential part of resource conservation and sustainable urban development[12]. The goals of reducing dependence on virgin resources, minimizing environmental degradation, and supporting long-term sustainability can be achieved by promoting reuse, high-quality recycling, and circular material flows within the construction industry[12].

### 3.3 Greenhouse Gas Emissions

Greenhouse gas emissions and climate change is closely linked to construction waste management[12]. During the lifecycle of construction materials, including extraction, manufacturing, transportation, waste collection, recycling, and landfill operations, emissions are generated that affect othe global climate. Therefore, reducing the carbon footprint of construction activities has become an important objective.

According to recent researches, landfill-oriented waste management systems contribute less than recycling-based practices in greenhouse gas production[12]. One major factor which results in the increase of carbon dioxide is the shipping of construction waste[12]. Moreover, manufacturing of cement and steel which are categorized as virgin materials have a substantial effect on energy consumption and carbon dioxide emissions worldwide.

A factor which decreases cumulative energy requirement and output of greenhouse gas is reprocessing concrete and steel. The production of virgin materials needs much more energy consumption than reclaimed ones, and this contribute even further to carbon dioxide emissions. On the contrary, in construction supply chains, applying recycling approaches and focusing on material recovery helps in reducing the global warming potential[12].

An important factor affecting the environmental impact of waste management systems is transportation distance effect[13]. Studies found that using localized recycling facilities can considerably reduce transportation costs and emissions by minimizing the distance between construction sites and recycling centers. This strategy can also be used to reduce the carbon footprint of construction activities[13].

In spite of the advantages that recycling systems provide, some researchers believe that their effectiveness depends on sorting efficiency, processing technology and energy consumption. Nevertheless, the overall view of the literature strongly suggests recycling, reuse, and waste minimization as effective approaches for reducing greenhouse gas emissions within the construction sector[12].

In summary, construction waste generates serious environmental impacts through water and soil pollution, depletion of natural resources, and increased greenhouse gas emissions[12]. The reviewed literature consistently demonstrates that sustainable waste management strategies — particularly recycling, reuse, and efficient material handling — can substantially reduce these environmental burdens and contribute to more sustainable construction practices[12].
